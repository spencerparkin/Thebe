How to Play Chinese Checkers Extreme

If you already know how to play the traditional game of Chinese Checkers, then there are only a few more things to know about this extreme variation of the game. Nevertheless, I will start at the beginning, because I not only explain the rules of the game here, but also how to use this software.

1 -- Starting a Game

You can begin a game by either joining a game already started by someone else, or by starting a new game yourself.

1.1 -- Hosting a New Game

Choosing Host Game from the Game menu brings up the following dialog.

Here, the Game Type refers to the type of board you're going to use, which also determines the maximum number of players that can participate in the game. The traditional board can host anywhere from 2 to 6 players.

There is no need to change the Host Address field unless there's a specific NIC you want to use. More likely, it's the Host Port field that will concern you. Choose a free port on your system, and one that isn't blocked by a fire-wall. Those who want to join your game will need to know your machine's host address and the port number you've chosen here.

The total number of players participating will be the sum of human and computer players, which must not exceed the total number of players supported by the chosen board type. Note that after a server is created, the software will automatically try to join the game as a human. If there are zero human players, then it will just spectate. If two or more human players are specified here, then the game can't progress until enough human players join the game from other instances of the program running elsewhere on the network (or even the same machine.)

1.2 -- Joining an Existing Game

Choosing Join Game from the Game menu brings up the following dialog.

Here, you just need to know the Host Address of the machine that's running the game server, and the Host Port that it's listening on for new connections. What color you are in the game just depends on the order in which people join.

2 -- Taking a Turn

Once a game is joined, you'll see indicators specifying what color you are, and when it's your turn.

You can see here it says "You are color Yellow" at the bottom, and "Waiting for color Yellow to take their turn."

2.1 -- Inspecting the Game Board

You only need a mouse with three buttons and a wheel to play this game. Click and drag with the left mouse button to shift the board around. Do this while holding the control key down to rotate the board about the focal point. Use the mouse wheel to zoom in and out. Do this while holding the control key down to tilt the board up and down.

2.2 -- Formulating Your Turn

Your turn consists of choosing one of your board pieces (a Yellow piece, in this case) and then describing how it should move along the board in a sequence of hops. You can either hop to an unoccupied adjacent location on the board, or you can hop over some other board piece (doesn't matter what color it is) to a location on the opposite side. In this latter case you are allowed to do an additional hop as long as it is also a jump over some other board piece. You can continue to do this, all in a single turn, as long as each additional hop jumps over another board piece.

To choose the piece you want to move, right-click on it. Doing so, a ring is placed around the piece to indicate its selection, as shown below.

3 -- The Goal of the Game

The goal of this variation of the game is the same as that of the traditional version, but with a bit of a twist. Traditionally, the goal is to get all of your pieces into the target zone directly across the board before your opponent does the same. That is, Yellow wants to move all of its pieces into the Red zone (where Red starts) before Red is able to move all of their pieces into the Yellow zone (where Yellow starts.)

In the extreme variation, the goal is to get all of your surviving pieces into the target zone. That's right, your pieces can be killed! Note that this presents one other way to win: being the last group of pieces on the board with one or more surviving pieces still in play. If all your pieces are killed (taken off the board), then you're out of contention for winning the game.

4 -- Some Considerations in Closing

That's basically all there is to it. You're now ready to play!

Note that in this variation of Chinese Checkers, it is not always to your advantage to kill off an opponent's piece. Remember, to win, they need only get their last surviving pieces into the target zone, even if they only have one left.
